# SUAS Veteran Crisis Q.R.F. — Brand Voice Guide

Companion to [`docs/branding-review.md`](branding-review.md),
[`docs/audience-personas.md`](audience-personas.md), and
[`docs/mission-vision-options.md`](mission-vision-options.md). This defines
the brand pillars (what we stand for), the tone of voice (how we sound), and
the elevator pitch (how we introduce ourselves to donors).

The voice already exists in fragments — the Families page ("You don't need
the right words — you need to stay"), the HALT section, the honest-claim
register. This guide names that voice so every future sentence can be written
in it, and so the fragments that *don't* match it ("crisis-prevention
infrastructure," "fewer missed cases") can be brought into line.

---

## 1. Brand pillars — five things we stand for

### Pillar 1 · Before, not after

**What it means.** Everything we do happens upstream of the crisis. The
whole ecosystem — 911, 988, ERs — is built to answer the worst moment; SUAS
exists so that moment arrives less often. We are the quick reaction force
that moves *before* the firefight.

- **Sounds like:** "Support should reach a veteran while the problem is
  still small." / "The warning signs were there for weeks. We connect them."
- **Never sounds like:** emergency-response promises ("Emergency services,
  one tap away"), rescue drama, or crisis countdown urgency.
- **Proof behind it:** the five-step early-support workflow; the signal
  system; the explicit "not emergency care" boundary.

### Pillar 2 · Connection is prevention

**What it means.** Isolation is where crises grow — so connection is not a
nice-to-have, it is the intervention. We don't deliver care; we keep
veterans connected to the people and help they already trust.

- **Sounds like:** "Isolation grows in silence. Checking in breaks it." /
  "A text from a buddy, at the right week, changes the month."
- **Never sounds like:** clinical treatment language, "case management" of
  human beings, or technology worship (the app is the wiring, not the point).
- **Proof behind it:** the trusted-circle model; consent-based alerts; peer
  responders.

### Pillar 3 · On your terms

**What it means.** The veteran holds the controls — who is in the circle,
what gets shared, what happens next. Dignity and agency are non-negotiable:
nobody becomes "a case," and nothing moves without consent.

- **Sounds like:** "Only the people you choose." / "Checking in isn't asking
  for help. It's staying connected — on your terms."
- **Never sounds like:** surveillance ("we monitor veterans"), pity ("broken
  heroes"), deficit framing, or decisions made *about* veterans instead of
  *by* them.
- **Proof behind it:** consent-based alerts, privacy-first design, the
  veteran-initiated participation model.

### Pillar 4 · Say only what's true

**What it means.** We claim exactly what we can prove, label every demo as
a demo, and publish what we cannot claim yet. In a space full of
overpromising, our honesty is not a disclaimer — it is a competitive
advantage and the foundation of every relationship we have.

- **Sounds like:** "Here's what we can claim today, and here's what we
  can't yet." / "The pilot exists to find out — and you'll get the report
  either way."
- **Never sounds like:** inflated impact numbers, "proven outcomes" before
  proof, unverifiable claims ("100% of donations"), or hype of any kind.
- **Proof behind it:** the honest-claim register; "Demo · sample data"
  labels; partners listed as "(seeking)."

### Pillar 5 · Show up with something real

**What it means.** Support is concrete or it is nothing: a meal, a ride, a
bed, a phone call, a follow-up that actually happens. We talk in nouns a
tired person can picture, not in abstractions.

- **Sounds like:** "A hot meal tonight. A ride tomorrow. A friend who calls
  back Thursday." / "Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired — meet the need behind
  the hard night."
- **Never sounds like:** "coordination workflows," "resource utilization,"
  "leveraging infrastructure" — plumbing words that make real help sound
  theoretical.
- **Proof behind it:** the HALT model; resource routing; responder
  follow-up logs.

## 2. Tone of voice — how we sound

### The one-line definition

> **A trusted squadmate at the kitchen table: steady, plain-spoken, honest
> about limits, and quietly certain that showing up early works.**

### The four tone qualities (in priority order)

1. **Steady** — calm and unhurried, never alarmist. We are the opposite of
   sirens. Crisis-adjacent copy especially stays level: short sentences,
   second person, clear next steps. Urgency is real in our cause, but our
   *voice* transmits steadiness — that's what a QRF sounds like.
2. **Plain-spoken** — 6th–8th-grade sentences, concrete nouns, active verbs.
   If a phrase wouldn't survive being said aloud at a VFW post or a kitchen
   table, rewrite it. Systems vocabulary is allowed only on
   partner/funder pages, never veteran- or family-facing.
3. **Honest** — we volunteer our limits before anyone asks ("not emergency
   care," "not clinically validated — the pilot exists to find out").
   Hedges live in dedicated, well-written boundary statements — not
   sprinkled as "(demo)" confetti through every sentence.
4. **Quietly hopeful** — hope grounded in mechanism, not sentiment: things
   get better because someone noticed early and showed up, not because we
   wish hard. Strengths-based always: veterans are capable people carrying
   heavy things, never broken objects of charity.

### What we are *not*

Not **urgent-alarmist** (violates safe messaging and our steadiness), not
**clinical** (we are not a treatment), not **corporate** (no synergy, no
stakeholders, no infrastructure in public copy), not **sentimental** (no
pity, no swelling-strings hero worship), not **jokey** (warmth yes, banter
no — the stakes are real).

### Tone dial by audience

The voice never changes; the dial does.

| Context | Dial setting | Example register |
|---|---|---|
| Veterans (B1/B2) | Peer-to-peer, maximum agency, zero fuss | "Sixty seconds. Your people. Your terms." |
| Families (B3) | Warmest setting, reassuring, role-giving | "You're not the case manager. You're the person who stays." |
| Volunteers (V1/V2) | Mission language, direct ask, clear lane | "A queue, a backstop, a team. Be the one who answers." |
| Donors (D1) | Candid, concrete, evidence-minded | "Fund the pilot that finds out — and get the report either way." |
| Institutions (D3, counties) | Most precise setting; technical terms allowed | "Consent architecture, escalation boundaries, pilot evaluation." |
| Crisis-adjacent copy | Calmest and most directive; always second person | "If you're in danger now: call 988, press 1, or text 838255." |

### Lexicon — words we use, words we avoid

| We say | We don't say |
|---|---|
| veterans, people, your people | cases, users, clients, the veteran population |
| check in, show up, reach out, connect | monitor, track, surveil, intervene |
| a hard week, a heavy season, carrying a lot | breaking point, the edge, the last straw, despair |
| early support, before the crisis | emergency (reserved for 911/988 only) |
| trusted circle, someone who cares | support network stakeholders |
| a meal, a ride, a bed, a follow-up | services, resources, deliverables (veteran-facing) |
| what we can claim today | industry-leading, proven (until proven) |
| died by suicide (if ever needed) | committed suicide, successful attempt |

## 3. The elevator pitch (donor-facing, ~30 seconds)

> **"Most veteran crises don't come out of nowhere. The warning signs show
> up for weeks — lost sleep, skipped meals, a guy going quiet — they just
> never connect in one place until it's too late. SUAS connects them. A
> veteran does a private, sixty-second check-in; when a hard week shows up,
> the people he trusts get a heads-up — with his consent — and real help
> follows: a meal, a ride, a bed, a friend who calls back. We're a
> California veteran nonprofit launching our first pilot — twenty-five to
> fifty veterans — to prove early connection keeps people out of crisis.
> Crisis response is funded. Prevention isn't. That's what your gift
> builds — so no one is left behind."**

*(~100 words ≈ 30 seconds at a natural pace.)*

**Why it's built this way:** it opens with the insight, not the org (the
listener nods before they hear a name); the mechanism is one concrete story
beat ("a heads-up — with his consent — and a meal, a ride, a bed"); the
honest stage framing becomes the *ask* ("a pilot to prove it") instead of a
weakness; "Crisis response is funded. Prevention isn't." gives the donor a
gap only they can fill; and it lands on the ethos line. Every sentence obeys
the pillars: before-not-after (1), connection (2), consent (3), honest
proof-seeking (4), concrete help (5).

### The 10-second version (for when there's no elevator)

> **"SUAS connects the early warning signs in a veteran's life so the people
> he trusts can show up before a hard week becomes a crisis. We're launching
> our first pilot now — help us prove prevention works."**

### One-breath answers to the two follow-ups donors always ask

- **"How is this different from the crisis line?"** — "The crisis line
  answers the worst moment — thank God it exists. We work weeks earlier, so
  fewer calls ever need to happen. We put its number on every page."
- **"Is it working?"** — "Honest answer: that's what the pilot is for. We
  publish what we can claim and what we can't yet — you'll get the
  post-pilot report either way."

## 4. Using this guide

- Every new sentence of public copy should trace to at least one pillar; if
  it traces to none, it probably belongs to a different organization.
- The tone dial changes by audience; the voice and lexicon never do.
- The elevator pitch is the canonical spoken intro; the mission statement
  (see mission-vision options, M2 recommended) is the canonical written one.
- When honesty and inspiration seem to conflict, they don't: state the limit
  plainly, then aim the aspiration at the vision ("No one left behind").
