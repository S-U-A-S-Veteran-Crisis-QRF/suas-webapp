# SUAS Veteran Crisis Q.R.F. — Mission & Vision Statement Options

Companion to [`docs/branding-review.md`](branding-review.md) and
[`docs/audience-personas.md`](audience-personas.md). Five mission options
(what we do today) and five vision options (the future we want to create),
each with the reasoning, trade-offs, and a recommended pairing.

**Ground rules every option obeys** (drawn from the brand audit):

1. **Honest register.** Mission statements describe what the org actually
   does today — connecting, coordinating, equipping — never unproven outcomes
   ("we prevent suicide"). Vision statements are where the aspiration lives;
   that split *is* the honest-claims culture applied to messaging.
2. **Safe messaging.** No fatalism ("breaking point," "the edge"), no
   crisis-as-inevitable framing (review finding W36). Isolation — not the
   veteran — is the enemy.
3. **Plain language.** Every option reads at roughly a 6th–8th-grade level
   and survives being said aloud by a volunteer at a VFW post. No
   "infrastructure," no "coordination workflows" (W9, W34).
4. **One sentence each.** Repeatable is the point (the site's current
   discipline with its core sentence is a strength — S3 — worth keeping).

## Mission statement options (what we do today)

### M1 — The Plain One

> **"SUAS helps veterans get support early — connecting them to the people
> they trust and the help they need before everyday struggles become
> crises."**

**Why it works.** It keeps the site's single best-proven asset — the core
sentence already repeated with discipline on the homepage, About page, and
metadata ("before everyday struggles become serious crises") — and simply
humanizes it: "people they trust" and "help they need" replace
"infrastructure." Zero learning curve for the org, zero jargon for the
public, and completely honest at pilot stage (helping and connecting is what
the org does today). **Trade-off:** the least distinctive of the five — it
describes the promise, not the unique mechanism.
**Best if:** you want maximum continuity and the safest general-public
statement.

### M2 — The Signal One *(recommended)*

> **"SUAS connects the scattered warning signs in a veteran's life — so
> someone who cares can reach out before a hard week becomes a crisis."**

**Why it works.** This is the only option that states the org's actual
differentiating insight: veteran crises are preceded by visible warning
signs that never connect in one place (the site's own problem statement,
`app/page.tsx:86-88`). "Connects the scattered warning signs" is the
mechanism; "someone who cares" is the trusted-circle model in three words;
"a hard week" keeps it non-fatalistic and relatable. It answers the question
the brand audit said goes unanswered (W25): *why does this org need to exist
when crisis lines already do?* Because crisis lines answer the worst moment
— nobody connects the early signals. Funders (persona D3) hear a fundable
gap; veterans (B1) hear something other than therapy. **Trade-off:**
slightly more abstract than M1 — "warning signs" requires a beat of thought.
**Best if:** you want the mission to carry the positioning argument itself.

### M3 — The QRF One

> **"SUAS builds the quick reaction force that moves before the crisis —
> private check-ins, a trusted circle you choose, and real help when it
> matters: a meal, a ride, a bed, a friend who follows up."**

**Why it works.** It resolves the brand's central paradox — a military
rapid-response name on a gentle prevention product — by *owning* the
metaphor: a QRF that deploys before the firefight. It finally makes the name
mean something (the audit's top naming finding, W1), it is
veteran-authentic in a way B1 and V1 will feel instantly, and it is the most
concrete option — "a meal, a ride, a bed" (the HALT vocabulary, the site's
best writing) makes the help tangible. **Trade-offs:** the longest option;
depends on shipping the acronym-expansion fix so "quick reaction force"
lands for civilian donors; the dispatch imagery must never drift back into
"emergency" language (W3). **Best if:** you commit to the QRF identity
everywhere — this mission and the name then reinforce each other instead of
fighting.

### M4 — The Shortest One

> **"SUAS keeps veterans connected to the people and help they trust —
> because isolation is where crises grow."**

**Why it works.** Thirteen working words of activity plus a because-clause
that teaches the theory of change in six more. It names the true enemy —
isolation, not the veteran, not "brokenness" — which is both safe-messaging
compliant and quietly destigmatizing: the veteran isn't the problem;
disconnection is. It's the easiest of the five to memorize, print on a
wristband, or say on a podcast. **Trade-off:** compresses away the mechanism
(no check-ins, no circle, no routing) — it needs the supporting copy around
it to explain *how*. **Best if:** you want a statement people actually
repeat verbatim.

### M5 — The People-Powered One

> **"SUAS equips the people veterans already trust — family, buddies, peer
> responders — to see the early signs and show up before a crisis."**

**Why it works.** It centers the org's real operating model: SUAS doesn't
deliver care, it activates the veteran's own people (the trusted-circle
architecture). That does three jobs at once — it recruits the volunteer
persona (V1 Ray hears "that's me"), it reassures the family persona (B3
Elena hears "I have a role"), and it makes the honest scope self-evident:
equipping supporters is exactly what a pre-clinical, consent-based platform
does. "Show up" is the warmest verb in the set. **Trade-off:** the veteran
is the object rather than the subject of the sentence — B1 may hear a tool
for *other people*. Pair it with veteran-facing copy that restores agency
("only the people you choose"). **Best if:** pilot recruitment of responders
and families is the near-term priority.

## Vision statement options (the future we want to create)

### V1 — The Universal One

> **"A world where no veteran faces a hard season alone."**

**Why it works.** Ten words, instantly understood by anyone, emotionally
true to the whole product (every feature exists to defeat isolation), and
fully safe-messaging compliant — "a hard season" acknowledges struggle
without predicting catastrophe. It is the vision-statement mirror of M4's
"isolation is where crises grow." **Trade-off:** generic enough that another
veteran org could claim it; it differentiates on warmth, not strategy.

### V2 — The Ethos One *(recommended)*

> **"No one left behind — every veteran connected to support long before
> crisis."**

**Why it works.** It promotes the brand's best buried asset. "No one is left
behind" — currently invisible in share metadata (`app/donate/page.tsx:8`,
finding W24) — is the warrior ethos every service member already carries;
using it as the vision makes veterans the *inheritors* of the promise they
once made to each other. The second clause converts the ethos from war-story
sentiment into this org's specific future: connection that arrives *long
before* crisis. It is simultaneously the tagline, the vision, and the
QRF-metaphor payoff. **Trade-off:** "left behind" is combat-adjacent
language — in context it reads as solidarity, not fatalism, but keep it away
from crisis-response copy.

### V3 — The Systemic One

> **"A country where veteran crises are prevented, not just answered."**

**Why it works.** One clean opposition — *prevented vs. answered* — that
locates SUAS precisely in the ecosystem: crisis lines, ERs, and 988 answer;
nobody prevents at scale. This is the vision as argument, and it's the
version institutional funders (D3) and counties can put in a grant memo. It
also honors, rather than competes with, the Veterans Crisis Line the site
rightly keeps prominent: answering must exist; SUAS works so it's needed
less. **Trade-off:** the most head-driven option — inspiring to funders and
partners, cooler for a grieving family member. Note "prevented" is safe
here because a vision describes an aspired future, not a claimed capability
— the honest-claim register stays intact.

### V4 — The Scale One

> **"Every community with its own quick reaction force for veterans — help
> that arrives before the crisis, every time."**

**Why it works.** It's the only option that contains the growth model: SUAS
scales community by community (county pilots → county networks), and this
vision lets every future chapter, county, and partner see themselves in it.
It pairs natively with mission M3, completing the reclaimed QRF identity,
and "every time" carries the reliability promise that makes a QRF a QRF.
**Trade-off:** depends on the QRF expansion being understood; "every time"
sets a high bar — appropriate for a vision, but never let it migrate into
present-tense program copy.

### V5 — The Culture-Change One

> **"A veteran culture where checking in is a habit, not a last resort."**

**Why it works.** The deepest version of the mission: the ultimate obstacle
isn't missing technology, it's the norm that says asking for connection
means weakness. This vision aims at the norm itself — make the check-in as
ordinary as PT, and the stigma B1 Marcus carries ("I'm fine; other guys had
it worse") simply has less to attach to. It frames veterans as a culture
capable of changing itself, which is respectful, strengths-based, and
different from every "save the veterans" vision in the space.
**Trade-off:** subtle — it inspires insiders (veterans, responders) more
than the general public, and it doesn't name crisis prevention at all.

## Recommended pairing and how to choose

**Recommended pair: M2 + V2.**

> **Mission:** SUAS connects the scattered warning signs in a veteran's life
> — so someone who cares can reach out before a hard week becomes a crisis.
>
> **Vision:** No one left behind — every veteran connected to support long
> before crisis.

Together they do the three jobs the brand audit said the current messaging
doesn't: the mission argues the differentiated gap (W25) in human language
(W9), and the vision surfaces the buried tagline as the emotional north star
(W24). If instead the org commits fully to the reclaimed-QRF identity,
**M3 + V4** is the coherent alternative pair — but only after the
acronym-expansion and demo-"emergency" fixes ship, so the metaphor is earned
rather than confusing.

**Decision test for the founder** — read each finalist aloud and ask:

1. Could a volunteer repeat it verbatim after hearing it once?
2. Is every claim in the *mission* true today, at pilot stage?
3. Would it still make sense (and stay honest) printed next to the crisis
   bar?
4. Does it sound like this org — and no other veteran org?

**Where they go once chosen** (per the audit's recommendations): mission on
the About page and homepage mission section (replacing "Crisis-prevention
infrastructure for veterans" as the public-facing H2), both statements on a
future leadership/About section, vision as the emotional close on the donate
page, and the tagline form of V2 in the footer lockup and default share
description.
